Tidal Energy

What is Tidal Energy?

Tidal power is a form of renewable energy generated from the movement of the tides. It is produced by the rise and fall of the ocean's tides and can be used to generate electricity. Tidal power plants are structures that are built to capture and convert the energy of the tides into electricity. They typically consist of a dam or barrier across an estuary, bay, or section of the ocean, which captures the water as the tide rises and falls. The water is then released through turbines, which spin to generate electricity.

Tidal power has been used for centuries to generate power for industry and homes. The earliest recorded use of tidal power dates back to the 11th century in Rome, when mills were powered by waterwheels. The first dedicated tidal power plants were built in France in the late 19th century.

History of tidal energy

The term "tidal power" was coined in the 1880s by Winston Churchill, who proposed using the tides of the Thames estuary to generate electricity. Since then, tidal power has been used in many countries around the world to generate electricity. Today, tidal power plants are being developed in countries such as the United Kingdom, Canada, and South Korea.

How much hydro energy is being used presently?

Currently, tidal power is estimated to generate around 0.3 terawatts (TW) of electricity worldwide. This is equivalent to around 1% of the world's total electricity production. The majority of this power is generated in the United Kingdom, Canada, and South Korea.

In many countries, tidal power is being replaced by wind and solar power as the preferred source of renewable energy. This is due to the fact that tidal power plants are expensive to build and maintain, and the power produced is not consistent due to the unpredictability of the tide. Additionally, the environmental impact of tidal power plants can be significant, as they can disrupt the natural flow of the sea and damage marine habitats.
